Dividing Europe: Berlin and the Iron Curtain

Key Idea

Europe Was Split in Two

After World War II, Europe did not return to normal. Instead, it became divided into two sides. Western Europe was influenced by the United States and democratic governments. Eastern Europe was controlled by the Soviet Union and communist systems.

This division created tension that defined the Cold War.

Berlin

A City Divided

Germany was split into four zones after the war, each controlled by a different Allied power. Even though Berlin was located inside Soviet-controlled territory, it was also divided into four sections.

This made Berlin one of the most important and tense locations in the Cold War.

Berlin became a symbol of the larger conflict between the United States and the Soviet Union.
Iron Curtain

A Divided Continent

Winston Churchill described the division of Europe as an "Iron Curtain." This was not a physical wall at first, but a political and ideological boundary.

On one side were democratic nations. On the other were communist states controlled by the Soviet Union.

This division limited movement, communication, and freedom between the two sides.
